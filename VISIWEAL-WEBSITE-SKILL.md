one page at a time, before moving to the next.

Page 1: Home

Page 8: Services — Corporate Restructuring
Page 9: Services — Feasibility Studies
Page 10: Insights / Blog
Page 11: Contact

### Page Sequencing Protocol

The audit and redraft process follows this sequence
for every page:

Step 1 — User submits the existing draft copy
for the page.

Step 2 — Full audit is conducted. Every issue
is flagged with:
- The exact copy that fails
- The specific standard it violates
  (Brand / Voice / SEO / Structure)
- Why it fails
- A short directional example showing
  what corrected copy could look like

Step 3 — User reviews the audit, confirms
the direction or redirects specific points.

Step 4 — Full redraft is produced only after
alignment is confirmed.

Step 5 — Redraft is reviewed and approved.

Step 6 — Move to the next page.

No page proceeds to redraft without explicit
user confirmation after the audit.
No page is skipped.
No two pages are audited simultaneously.

## SECTION 7 — SERVICE PAGES FRAMEWORK

Each of the six service pages must follow this
content structure:

1. Page Headline
   — One line. Directly states the value delivered.
   Not the service name. The outcome.

2. Opening Statement (2–3 sentences)
   — What this service is, who it is for, and
   why it matters at the enterprise level.

3. The Decision Context (1 paragraph)
   — The specific situation or inflection point
   that brings a client to need this service.
   Name the pressure. Be specific. MENA-anchored.

4. What VisiWeal Does (prose, not bullets)
   — How the service is delivered. Senior-level.
   Execution-focused. No generic descriptions.

5. What Clients Gain (3–4 outcomes maximum)
   — Framed around value creation, protection,
   or realization. Not features. Outcomes.

6. Who This Is For
   — Specific audience. Not "businesses of all sizes."
   Name the type of client. Name the situation.

7. CTA
   — One clear next step. Consistent with the
   homepage CTA unless a stronger alternative
   is justified by the service context.

### Service-Specific Positioning Notes

M&A Advisory:
Position around transaction value and execution
certainty. The key message is that most deals
that fail, fail in execution — not in intent.
VisiWeal closes that gap.

Financial Advisory:
Position around the CFO-level perspective brought
to non-CFO environments. Strategic financial
counsel, not accounting services.

Digital Transformation:
Position exclusively through the lens of enterprise
value. Technology is the mechanism. Value creation
is the purpose. Never lead with technology.
Always lead with the business outcome.

Fractional CFO:
Position around the gap between what a business
needs at CFO level and what it can sustain full-time.
This is not outsourcing. It is senior financial
leadership engaged at the right moment.

Corporate Restructuring:
Position around value preservation under pressure.
The message is not distress — it is discipline.
Restructuring is a strategic tool, not a last resort.

Feasibility Studies:
Position around decision confidence. Before capital

## SECTION 8 — FOUNDER PAGE FRAMEWORK

The Founder page is not a biography.
It is a credibility document.

It must answer three questions a CFO or board member
asks before engaging an advisor:

1. Has this person done this before, at scale,
   in markets I recognize?
2. Do they understand the specific complexity
   of my situation — family business, MENA,
   cross-border, regulatory fragmentation?
3. Can I trust their judgment, not just their CV?

Structure:

1. Opening Statement (not a job title introduction)
   — Begin with a perspective, a position, or
   an observation that demonstrates judgment.
   Not "Sleiman El-Khoury has X years of experience."

2. Career Narrative (prose, not a timeline)
   — Siemens → Nokia Siemens Networks → PwC ecosystem
   → Group CFO roles → VisiWeal.
   Connect the roles. Show the arc. Show what
   each phase built toward.

3. What This Means for Clients
   — Translate the experience into client value.
   What does 35 years across 17+ markets mean
   for a family business owner in Riyadh or
   a shareholder in Beirut?

4. The VisiWeal Founding Premise
   — Why 2015. Why independent. Why boutique.
   This is the trust anchor.

5. Languages & Markets
   — English, Arabic, French.
   GCC, Levant, international.

6. CTA
   — Invitation to a direct conversation.
   Not a generic contact form reference.

## SECTION 11 — HUMAN WRITING STANDARDS

These rules apply to every word produced for
the VisiWeal website — page copy, service
descriptions, article content, CTA text,
and meta descriptions.

### The Non-Negotiable Core Rules

Sound like an advisor, not a template.
Be specific. Always. No exceptions.
Vary sentence length. Short. Then longer.
Cut every phrase that has appeared on LinkedIn
this week.
Name the city. Name the law. Name the institution.
Take a position. Then defend it with evidence.
Every bullet point must earn its place.
Every subheading must make the reader want to
keep reading.
Data punctuates arguments. It does not replace them.
If it could have been written about any industry
in any country — rewrite it for MENA and for now.

### Sentence Rhythm

Use the 1-3-1 pattern throughout:

Short. (5–8 words. Maximum impact.)

Then a medium-length sentence that builds on that
point and adds context, data, or nuance to what
was just said.

Then a longer, more developed sentence that either
introduces a complication, adds a regional example,
or moves the analysis forward in a way that requires
the reader to stay with you until the end.

Then short again. Repeat.

Uniform sentence length is the clearest signal
of AI-generated content. Vary it deliberately.

### Paragraph Architecture

Every paragraph does ONE of these jobs:
- Makes a claim
- Supports a claim with evidence
- Complicates or qualifies a claim
- Transitions to the next claim

Maximum paragraph length: 4 sentences
Ideal paragraph length: 2–3 sentences
Minimum paragraph length: 1 sentence (impact only)

### Subheading Style

Subheadings must read like chapter titles in a book
a senior executive would actually pick up.

WRONG:
"Overview of the Current Situation"
"Key Considerations for Businesses"
"Conclusion and Final Thoughts"

RIGHT:
"The Numbers That Should Concern Every CFO in Cairo"
"Why the First 100 Days After a Deal Define Everything"
"When the Family Table Becomes the Boardroom"
"The Hidden Cost Most Feasibility Studies Miss"

Every subheading must make the reader want to
read the section. It teases the insight without
giving it away entirely.

### Banned Phrases (Zero Tolerance)

Never use:
"In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
"In the ever-changing world of..."
"As the MENA region continues to evolve..."
"It is no secret that..."
"Now more than ever..."
"Navigating the complexities of..."
"Whether you are a CEO or an entrepreneur..."
"Certainly" or "Absolutely" to open a sentence
"This article will explore..."
"In this piece, we will discuss..."
"In conclusion, it is clear that..."
"At its core..."
"It goes without saying..."

And all banned filler phrases listed in Section 4.

### Word Frequency Caps Per Page

"Critical" — maximum 2 uses
"Significant" — maximum 2 uses
"Important" — maximum 2 uses
"Key" — maximum 3 uses
"Strategy" / "Strategic" — maximum 4 uses
"Opportunity" — maximum 4 uses
"Transformation" — maximum 3 uses
"Growth" — maximum 4 uses
"Region" / "Regional" — maximum 5 uses
"Businesses" — maximum 5 uses
"Advisory" — maximum 3 uses (outside headings)

### Regional Fluency Requirements

Name cities, not just countries:
"Riyadh's private sector" not just "Saudi Arabia"
"Cairo's mid-market" not just "Egypt"
"Beirut's holding groups" not just "Lebanon"

Reference real institutions naturally:
DIFC, ADGM, SAMA, Tadawul, EGX, ADIA, Mubadala,
PIF, CMA, Casablanca Finance City, Jordan's ASE

Acknowledge the diversity within MENA:
The UAE is not Iraq.
Morocco is not Qatar.
Lebanon's business environment in 2025 is
categorically different from Oman's.

Avoid Western idioms:
Do not use: "knock it out of the park",
"ballpark figure", "hit the ground running",
"get buy-in", "circle back", "bandwidth"

Replace with: "execute with precision",
"approximately", "act immediately",
"build consensus", "follow up on", "capacity"

### Sensitive Country Contexts

Lebanon:
Acknowledge the banking crisis and economic
contraction as established fact — not drama.
Focus on resilience, adaptation, and the
still-significant private sector.

Iraq:
Position as an emerging market with genuine
opportunity in infrastructure, energy, and
financial services. Acknowledge governance risks
only where directly relevant. Do not dwell.

Egypt:
The IMF reform program and devaluation are context,
not the story. The story is the scale of the market,
the growing private sector, and the opportunity in
M&A and digitalization.

Jordan:
Economically sophisticated and often overlooked.
Strong professional services sector and growing
tech ecosystem. Write with the same depth as GCC.

Saudi Arabia:
Vision 2030 is real and powerful. Reference it
where it genuinely shapes the topic. Do not
mention it in every paragraph.

UAE:
DIFC and ADGM as financial hubs. Position the
UAE as the regional headquarters of serious
capital decisions — not just a convenient base.

## SECTION 13 — AUDIT PROTOCOL

This section governs how every page audit is
conducted. It is the operating procedure for
the entire website review process.

### Audit Trigger

The audit begins when the user submits the
existing draft copy for a specific page with
the following trigger:

"Audit [Page Name] — existing copy below."

No audit begins without the existing copy
being provided in full.

### Audit Output Structure

For every page submitted, the audit produces
the following output in this exact order:

---

PAGE AUDIT: [Page Name]

OVERALL ASSESSMENT
A 3–5 sentence summary of the page's current
state. What it does well (if anything). What its
primary failures are. How far it is from the
VisiWeal standard.

---

ISSUE LOG

Each issue is documented as follows:

ISSUE [Number]
Category: [Brand / Voice / SEO / Structure]
Severity: [Critical / Major / Minor]
Location: [Section or line reference]
Current Copy: "[exact text that fails]"
Why It Fails: [Specific explanation referencing
the relevant standard from this skill]
Directional Example: "[A short example showing
what corrected copy could look like — not the
final redraft, but enough to show the direction]"

---

Issues are listed in order of severity:
Critical first, then Major, then Minor.

Critical: Directly contradicts brand positioning,
creates wrong impression of the firm, or contains
significant SEO errors that would harm ranking.

Major: Weakens the message, uses banned language,
misses a key audience signal, or contains
structural problems that reduce conversion.

Minor: Tone inconsistency, suboptimal word choice,
formatting issue, or missed SEO opportunity that
does not damage but does not optimize.

---

SEO AUDIT SUMMARY
Separate from the copy issues.
Documents:
- Current meta title (if provided) and issues
- Current meta description (if provided) and issues
- H1 and heading structure assessment
- Keyword presence and gaps
- URL structure (if applicable)
- Internal linking opportunities identified
- Recommended primary keyword for this page
- Recommended meta title
- Recommended meta description

---

AUDIT COMPLETE.
Confirm direction on the issues above before
redraft begins. Note any points where you want
to redirect the suggested approach.

---

### Post-Audit Protocol

After the audit is delivered:

User reviews each issue and the directional
examples provided.

User confirms which directions to proceed with,
and flags any points of disagreement or
redirection.

Only after explicit confirmation does the
full redraft begin.

The redraft is then produced as complete,
publication-ready copy for the page.

After the redraft is reviewed and approved,
the next page audit begins.

No page is skipped.
No two pages are in audit simultaneously.
No redraft is produced without audit confirmation.

## SECTION 14 — FINAL QUALITY TEST

Before any copy is considered complete — whether
audit directional examples, redrafted page copy,
or insights articles — run this test.

Ask:

Does this strengthen the perception of VisiWeal
as a trusted advisor?

Does this reinforce enterprise value creation?

Would a CEO, shareholder, board member, or investor
in Dubai, Riyadh, Beirut, or Baghdad find this
credible?

Would this positioning be consistent with a boutique
advisory firm that has handled a $500 million
transaction?

Does it sound like a real person wrote this?

Is there at least one sentence a senior executive
could imagine forwarding to a colleague?

Is every claim specific enough that it could not
have been written about a different firm in a
different market?

If the answer to any of these is not a clear yes —
rewrite.
